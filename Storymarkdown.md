## Oriental Shorthairs: the Siamese in designer genes

Oriental Shorthairs are among the most engaging, intelligent, and personality-driven cat breeds in the world. CFA describes them as "extremely bright" — they learn to walk on leashes, play fetch, and perform tricks like sit and high-five. Breeders and owners consistently call them **"dog-like"** because they greet owners at the door, follow them room to room, ride on shoulders, and demand constant interaction. TICA's breed description notes they "love everyone and love a party," happily jumping from lap to lap when guests arrive.

Their vocality is legendary. Oriental Shorthairs don't meow quietly — they produce a distinctive **honk** (earning the nickname "honking cat"), along with chirps, raspy commentary, and persistent conversation about everything happening in the house. They are famously mischievous: examining toothbrushes, "helping" select breakfast from the refrigerator, and getting into everything they can reach. They are also deeply sensitive cats that get their feelings hurt easily, bonding especially strongly with one person while remaining social with everyone.

The breed is genetically identical to Siamese cats — they share the same body type, the same personality, and belong to the same TICA/CFA breed group. The sole genetic difference is the colorpoint gene: Siamese carry two copies of the recessive `cs` gene (producing the pointed pattern), while Orientals carry the dominant `C` gene, distributing color across the entire body. This single genetic distinction produces an astonishing **300+ color and pattern combinations** — solids, tabbies, tortoiseshells, smokes, and bicolors — compared to the Siamese's limited pointed palette. Both breeds share the distinctive svelte, tubular body; wedge-shaped head; strikingly large ears; and long, whippy tail. Males typically weigh **8–12 pounds**, females under 8, and kittens mature slowly, reaching full development at 18–24 months.

### Why Oriental Shorthairs are genuinely hard to find

Oriental Shorthairs are not the rarest breed in existence, but they are **significantly harder to find than popular breeds** like Bengals, Maine Coons, or Ragdolls. The breed did not make CFA's top 15 most registered breeds list in 2024. Multiple sources describe the difficulty: Catster advises buyers to "expect to travel a decent distance," and one TICA search returned just 14 breeders who had signed the TICA code of ethics. Most dedicated Oriental breeders are small, home-based operations producing only **3–4 litters per year**, and waitlists are standard practice.

Several factors keep breeder numbers low. The breed demands deep knowledge of complex color genetics across 600+ combinations. Responsible breeding requires extensive genetic testing. Kittens need intensive socialization and must stay with breeders until **12–16 weeks** (some breeders hold until 4 months). Small litter sizes typical of Siamese-type breeds further limit availability. The breed is almost never found in shelters or rescues.

### Pricing and what buyers should expect to pay

Pet-quality Oriental Shorthair kittens from reputable breeders typically sell for **$600–$1,500**, with $850 being a common listing price on marketplace sites. Show and breeder-quality kittens command **$2,000–$3,500**, with champion bloodlines at the top of that range. Females cost $200–$500 more than males due to breeding potential, and rare colors (pure white, lilac, chocolate) command premium prices. Lifetime ownership costs are estimated at **$13,000–$17,000**.

Buyers should understand that Oriental Shorthairs are not low-maintenance pets. They require companionship — TICA states flatly that "an Oriental Shorthair does not do well as an only cat." They need a pet companion at their activity level, ideally another Oriental or Siamese. They are high-energy natural athletes who need cat trees, food puzzles, and daily interactive play. They show weight gain immediately — "even after indulging in one day of over eating," per Hill's Pet. They are best suited to work-from-home households, multi-pet families, and homes with older children.

### Health considerations every buyer and breeder must know

The most serious breed-specific concern is **hepatic amyloidosis** — abnormal protein deposits primarily affecting the liver, which can cause organ failure and sudden death in cats aged 1–7 years. The heritability is estimated at **0.56**, indicating a strong genetic component. Critically, **no genetic test currently exists** for this condition; responsible breeders manage it by removing affected cats and close relatives from breeding programs and monitoring family history. Research at the University of Missouri's Feline Genetics Lab is ongoing.

**Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA)** is the second major concern, causing progressive blindness starting around 1.5–2 years of age. Unlike amyloidosis, a **genetic test IS available**, and responsible breeders can identify and remove carriers from their programs. Additional health considerations include congenital heart defects (regular echocardiographic screening recommended), dental disease related to their narrow facial structure, and elevated risk for lymphoma, asthma, diabetes, and Feline Hyperesthesia Syndrome. With proper breeding and care, Orientals typically live **12–15 years**, with some reaching 20+.

## Serengeti cats: wild beauty, zero wild blood

The Serengeti is **one of the rarest domestic cat breeds on earth**, with a total worldwide population estimated at only several hundred animals. Created in 1994 by conservation biologist Karen Sausman at Kingsmark Cattery in California, the breed exists to answer a provocative question: can you create a cat that looks like an African Serval without using any wild blood at all? The answer is yes — through crossing **Bengal fathers with Oriental Shorthair mothers**.

From Bengals, the Serengeti inherits its spotted coat, large eyes, wide nose, and shiny, thick fur. From Oriental Shorthairs, it gets its extraordinarily long legs, elongated torso, massive round-tipped ears, and vocal personality. The result is a striking cat with **the longest legs of any domestic breed** — capable of jumping 7 feet in the air — wearing a spotted coat in gold, black, grey, or silver. Males weigh **10–15 pounds** and females 8–12. Their large, round-tipped ears sit directly on top of the skull and equal the length of the head, creating the unmistakable silhouette of a wild African cat.

The temperament is pure domestic magic. Serengetis are nicknamed **"Velcro cats"** because they attach themselves to their owners and follow them everywhere. They are confident, social, vocal, and deeply affectionate — the opposite of the unpredictable temperament that can come with actual wild-blood hybrids. As VioVet states, "Unlike the Savannah, the Serengeti contains no wild blood. For this reason, many consider it a more amiable breed that is better suited to domestic living."

### Why being a Serengeti breeder in the US matters enormously

There are likely **fewer than 5–10 active Serengeti breeders in the entire United States**. GoKitty.com's Serengeti listing consistently shows zero kittens available, with only 19 "Future Parents Following." The breed remains classified as a **TICA "Preliminary New Breed"** — the only registry worldwide that accepts them. It is not recognized by CFA, GCCF, or any other major registry. The breed is still in active development after 30+ years, and new ethical breeding programs directly impact its trajectory toward full championship status.

The market opportunity is significant. Serengeti kittens sell for **$600–$3,000**, with top-quality kittens commanding the high end. Waiting lists for rare breeds like this typically extend **a year or more**. Demand for exotic-looking domestic cats continues growing, particularly as Savannah cats face increasing legal restrictions across the country. Blue Sky Cattery would be entering a market with virtually no competition and strong, unmet demand.

### The Serengeti advantage over Savannah cats

This comparison is one of the most powerful selling points for the breed. Savannah cats are direct hybrids of domestic cats and wild African Servals. Serengetis achieve a similar wild aesthetic through **100% domestic breeding** — and this distinction has massive practical implications:

Serengetis are **legal everywhere** — no state bans, no city restrictions, no county ordinances. Savannahs are outright banned in Georgia, Hawaii, Nebraska, and Rhode Island, restricted by generation in roughly 18 states, and prohibited in major cities including Denver, New York City, and Seattle. Serengetis never require exotic animal permits. Any standard veterinarian can treat them. There is zero risk of confiscation. Their temperament is fully predictable and consistently domestic — no litter box refusal, no aggression issues, no need for outdoor enclosures. And they cost significantly less than early-generation Savannahs, which can exceed $20,000 for F1 animals.

For Blue Sky Cattery, the story practically writes itself: "All the wild beauty. None of the wild blood. Legal everywhere. Loved by everyone."

### What educated buyers look for and what raises red flags

Reputable breeders are identified by **TICA/CFA registration** (verifiable on organization websites), written health guarantees with clear terms for genetic conditions, legally binding contracts with spay/neuter agreements and return policies, complete vaccination records from a licensed veterinarian, genetic testing documentation for both parents, an application process that screens buyers (reputable breeders interview buyers as carefully as buyers evaluate breeders), kittens held until at least **12–16 weeks**, and lifetime support commitments.

Red flags that educated buyers watch for include always-available kittens (suggests overbreeding), no registration papers, no contract or health guarantee, kittens released before 12 weeks, pressure to buy quickly, self-administered vaccines without vet records, and breeding more than two unrelated breeds. Home-raised kittens — sometimes called "raised underfoot" — is one of the strongest selling points in purebred cat marketing. Kittens raised this way develop confident, well-adjusted personalities, while kittens from cage-based commercial operations frequently display fearful, feral-like behavior that can persist for life. ## Nutrition as a trust signal: Loyall Life, supplements, and homemade pâté

A cattery's feeding program communicates volumes about the quality of care kittens receive. Top breeders dedicate entire website pages to nutrition, name specific products and explain why they chose them, and include feeding instructions in their adoption contracts.

**Loyall Life Cat & Kitten Salmon & Ocean Fish** is manufactured by Nutrena, owned by Cargill — one of the world's largest food corporations. The formula features **real salmon as the first ingredient**, **31% crude protein**, and contains no corn, wheat, soy, by-products, or artificial preservatives. Its standout feature is **TruMune**, a proprietary postbiotic ingredient backed by over 425 controlled research studies that supports immune function and gut microbiome health — particularly valuable in a multi-cat breeding environment. The formula includes probiotics (five live cultures), prebiotics, DHA for brain and vision development, omega fatty acids for coat health, and supplemental taurine. Critically, it is an **all-life-stages formula**, meaning pregnant queens, nursing mothers, growing kittens, and adult cats can all eat the same food — simplifying cattery management enormously.

### Why taurine supplementation is non-negotiable for breeders

Taurine is an amino acid that cats **cannot synthesize in adequate quantities** — they require daily dietary intake. Deficiency causes devastating consequences: **dilated cardiomyopathy** (the most common heart disease in cats before taurine supplementation became standard), **feline central retinal degeneration** leading to irreversible blindness, reproductive failure including small litters and fetal abnormalities, stunted kitten growth, and immune dysfunction. Breeding cats have higher taurine demands due to pregnancy and lactation. Even though commercial foods contain taurine, breeders supplement as an insurance policy because cooking and processing degrade taurine content. Supplementation is considered extremely safe with no toxicity concerns, and therapeutic doses of **250 mg twice daily** can reverse early-stage DCM.

### The lysine conversation requires nuance

L-Lysine supplementation is widespread among breeders — over 90% of veterinarians in cat hospitals have recommended it, primarily to manage Feline Herpesvirus-1 (FHV-1), which is especially prevalent in multi-cat environments. Typical dosages are **250 mg daily for kittens** and **500 mg daily for adults**. However, a landmark 2015 systematic review in BMC Veterinary Research concluded there is no scientific evidence for lysine's efficacy against FHV-1, and some studies suggested it could worsen outcomes. The scientific picture is genuinely conflicted. If presented on the website, lysine supplementation is best framed as part of a **holistic wellness approach under veterinary guidance** rather than as a specific antiviral treatment.

### Homemade pâté signals extraordinary dedication

A cattery that makes its own fresh food communicates something powerful: these cats are treated as family, not inventory. Homemade pâté gives the breeder complete control over ingredients — human-grade proteins, organ meats rich in natural taurine, no fillers or preservatives. Fresh food retains more nutritional integrity than heavily processed commercial options and can be customized for pregnant queens, growing kittens, or specific dietary needs. The time investment alone is significant: sourcing ingredients, grinding meat, measuring supplements, portioning, and freezing. This level of effort clearly separates a premium cattery from operations cutting corners. On the website, the key message is simple: "We know exactly what goes into every meal our cats eat — because we make it ourselves."

## Biosecurity: why the best catteries keep their doors closed

The no-visitor policy is **standard practice among reputable breeders worldwide**, and for life-or-death reasons. A cattery is a nursery where kittens with immature immune systems develop, and visitors represent one of the highest-risk vectors for introducing deadly pathogens.

**Feline panleukopenia** (feline distemper) has a mortality rate exceeding **90% in unvaccinated kittens**. The virus can be carried on shoes, clothing, and hands — and survives in the environment for **over a year**, resistant to many common disinfectants. A visitor who walked through a contaminated area months ago could track the virus into a cattery without knowing. Ringworm spores remain infective for **up to 9 months** and can devastate a cattery, requiring weeks of treatment and complete interruption of breeding programs. Feline calicivirus is carried by **25–40% of cats in multi-cat environments**, and Feline Herpesvirus has infected over 80% of the cat population. Kittens don't complete their full FVRCP vaccination series until **16–20 weeks of age** — meaning for most of their time in the cattery, they lack full immune protection.

### Framing the policy as proof of quality, not a red flag

The most effective catteries reframe their closed-door policy as evidence of superior care. The language that works best positions the cattery as a **nursery** — "just like a hospital nursery doesn't allow unrestricted visitors, we protect our most vulnerable residents." The messaging should lead with the "why" (kitten health), cite specific threats (panleukopenia's 90% mortality rate, viruses surviving on shoes for over a year), emphasize the vulnerability window (kittens aren't fully vaccinated until 16 weeks), and immediately offer alternatives.

Those alternatives are critical for building trust without physical access. **Live video calls** (FaceTime/Zoom) are the single most effective trust-builder, allowing buyers to see kittens, parents, and the cattery environment in real time. Weekly photo and video updates showing kitten development, an active social media presence with behind-the-scenes content, complete health records and genetic testing results shared transparently, references from previous buyers, and verifiable TICA registration all provide comprehensive proof of legitimacy. Most closed catteries welcome buyers to visit in person at pickup time — giving them the opportunity to see the home environment when their kitten is fully vaccinated and ready to go.

A suggested framing for the website: *"We operate as a closed cattery — and this is one of the reasons our kittens go home healthy and thriving. Deadly viruses like feline panleukopenia can be carried on shoes and clothing without any visible signs, and this virus alone has a 90% mortality rate in unvaccinated kittens. We believe in full transparency: we offer live video calls, weekly photo updates, complete health records, and we welcome you to see our home when you pick up your kitten."*
